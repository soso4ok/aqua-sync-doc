AquaSync Developer Documentation

A Developer-Focused Deep Dive into EU Space Data and Water Monitoring

📖 About This Guide

This documentation explains how AquaSync is built on top of EU space infrastructure (Copernicus, Galileo, EGNOS) and the Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem (CDSE). It assumes no prior Earth Observation or biotechnology background and explains every term from a software developer's perspective.

What is AquaSync?

AquaSync is a data pipeline that combines two very different data sources to monitor water quality:

🛰️

"Macro" Data: Satellites

Copernicus satellites take repeated measurements of Earth's surface. Think of it as a global camera taking photos in different wavelengths every few days. We process these to detect water pollution patterns.

📱

"Micro" Data: Citizens

People on the ground take geotagged photos of water bodies. These provide "ground truth" - actual visual confirmation of what the satellite sees from 700km above.

The Problem We're Solving

Satellites have a fundamental limitation called "pixel pollution":

🔍 What is Pixel Pollution?

A satellite pixel might be 10×10 meters. Near coastlines or rivers, a single pixel often contains both land and water. This "mixed pixel" makes it impossible to detect small-scale chemical spills or early-stage algal blooms. The land reflectance contaminates the water signal.

Meanwhile, citizens see pollution on the ground but have no way to report it with proper verification. AquaSync bridges this gap by cross-referencing satellite anomalies with verified citizen reports.

High-Level Data Flow

The "aha moment" is when a citizen report appears on the map exactly on top of a satellite-detected anomaly, proving that both data sources agree:

  1. Satellite detects anomaly → Suspicion Map shows "something unusual here"
  2. Citizen snaps photo → Geotagged, timestamped evidence uploaded
  3. Data fusion → Backend checks if citizen coordinates intersect satellite anomaly
  4. Alert generated → If both agree, high-priority alert for authorities

Earth Observation (EO) for Developers

If you've never worked with satellite data, think of it as a REST API that returns images and measurements instead of JSON.

What Satellites Actually Measure

Satellites don't just take "photos." They measure electromagnetic radiation at different wavelengths:

Type What It Measures Developer Analogy
Optical (Sentinel-2) Reflected sunlight in visible + infrared bands Like a camera with 13 color channels instead of RGB
Radar/SAR (Sentinel-1) Microwave backscatter (satellite sends signal, measures echo) Like sonar, but with radio waves. Works through clouds and at night.
Ocean Color (Sentinel-3) 21 spectral bands optimized for water Lower resolution but more "color depth" for water analysis

Key Terms Explained

📦 Product Levels (L0, L1, L2)

Level 0: Raw sensor data (you never use this)
Level 1: Radiometrically corrected + georeferenced (basic image)
Level 2: Atmospherically corrected ("what the ground actually looks like" vs "what the sensor saw through atmosphere")

For water quality, always use Level 2 (L2A) products - the atmosphere has been "removed" from the measurements.

🎨 Spectral Bands

Instead of RGB, satellites have many "bands" - each capturing a specific wavelength range. Sentinel-2 has 13 bands:

  • B01 - Coastal aerosol (443nm) - useful for haze correction
  • B02 - Blue (490nm)
  • B03 - Green (560nm)
  • B04 - Red (665nm)
  • B05-B07 - Red-edge (705-783nm) - critical for vegetation/algae
  • B08 - NIR (842nm) - Near-infrared, strongly absorbed by water
  • B11-B12 - SWIR (1610-2190nm) - Short-wave infrared

Water strongly absorbs NIR. This is why comparing Green vs NIR tells us "is this pixel water or land?"

📐 Spatial Resolution

How big is one pixel on the ground?

  • Sentinel-2: 10m (RGB, NIR), 20m (red-edge, SWIR), 60m (atmospheric bands)
  • Sentinel-3: 300m (designed for oceans/large lakes, not small rivers)
  • Sentinel-1: 5-20m (radar)

A 10m pixel means you can't see anything smaller than 10×10 meters. A car is invisible; a football field is a few pixels.

⏱️ Temporal Resolution (Revisit Time)

How often does the satellite pass over the same spot?

  • Sentinel-2: Every 2-3 days at mid-latitudes (but may be cloudy)
  • Sentinel-1: Every 6 days (works through clouds)

Cloud-free images might only be available every few weeks. This is why radar (SAR) is crucial for flood monitoring.

GNSS & Positioning for Developers

GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite Systems) provides positioning and timing. In Europe, the main systems are Galileo and the augmentation system EGNOS.

Why Positioning Matters for Water Monitoring

EO tells us WHAT is happening (e.g., turbidity at a pixel)
GNSS tells us WHERE and WHEN a field photo was taken

Without precise, authenticated positioning, a malicious actor could fake coordinates to misdirect enforcement or hide illegal discharges.

GPS vs Galileo vs EGNOS

System Origin Typical Accuracy Special Feature
GPS USA ~3-5 meters Most widely supported, no authentication
Galileo EU ~1m (Open), <20cm (HAS) HAS corrections + OSNMA authentication
EGNOS EU (Regional) ~1m with integrity Integrity information for aviation safety

Key Concepts

🎯 RTK vs PPP

RTK (Real-Time Kinematic): Requires a nearby base station + cellular connection. Very accurate (~2cm) but infrastructure-dependent.
PPP (Precise Point Positioning): Uses satellite-broadcast corrections. Works anywhere but needs convergence time (~5 min).

Galileo HAS uses PPP - crucial for remote areas where there's no cellular coverage (e.g., river basins, disaster zones).

🔐 Spoofing

An attacker broadcasts fake GNSS signals to make a receiver report the wrong location. This is why OSNMA (authentication) matters - it cryptographically signs the navigation messages so receivers can verify they're genuine.

Sentinel Satellite Missions

The EU Copernicus programme operates several Sentinel missions. For water monitoring, we primarily use Sentinel-1, -2, and -3.

Mission Comparison

Mission Sensor Type Resolution Best For
Sentinel-1 SAR (Radar) 5-20m Flood mapping (works through clouds)
Sentinel-2 MSI (Optical, 13 bands) 10-60m Water quality, land cover
Sentinel-3 OLCI (21 bands) 300m Large lakes, oceans, color analysis

Sentinel-2: The Workhorse for Water Quality

Sentinel-2 carries the MSI (MultiSpectral Instrument) with 13 bands specifically chosen to detect vegetation health, water, and land features.

Band Reference

Band Wavelength Resolution Use in Water Analysis
B01 443nm (Coastal) 60m Aerosol correction, Chlorophyll
B02 490nm (Blue) 10m Cyanobacteria detection
B03 560nm (Green) 10m Water detection (NDWI numerator)
B04 665nm (Red) 10m Sediment, organic matter
B05-B07 705-783nm (Red-edge) 20m Algal bloom detection (phycocyanin peak)
B08 842nm (NIR) 10m Water detection (NDWI denominator)
B11-B12 1610-2190nm (SWIR) 20m Moisture, built-up areas

Sentinel-1: Radar for All-Weather Monitoring

SAR (Synthetic Aperture Radar) sends microwave pulses and measures the echo. Unlike optical sensors:

  • ✅ Works through clouds, smoke, and at night
  • ✅ Water surfaces appear very dark (specular reflection away from sensor)
  • ⚠️ Urban areas create "double-bounce" - floods in cities look bright, not dark

SAR Pre-processing Pipeline

Raw SAR data needs several corrections before use:

SAR Processing Chain 
1. Apply Orbit File      → Correct satellite position using precise ephemerides
2. Thermal Noise Removal → Remove sensor background noise
3. Radiometric Calibration → Convert DN to backscatter coefficient (σ⁰)
4. Speckle Filtering     → Remove "salt-and-pepper" noise (Lee/Gamma filter, 5×5 window)
5. Terrain Correction    → Fix geometric distortions using a DEM
6. dB Conversion         → σ⁰_dB = 10 × log₁₀(σ⁰_linear)

Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem (CDSE)

CDSE is the EU's cloud-native platform for Earth Observation data. Think of it as "AWS for satellite data" - instead of downloading terabytes of files, you send processing scripts to where the data lives.

The Paradigm Shift

❌ Old Way (Open Access Hub)

Download 1GB SAFE files → Process locally → Store results. Slow, bandwidth-heavy, requires big local storage.

✅ New Way (CDSE)

Send evalscript + AOI + time range → Server processes → Returns 50KB PNG or JSON stats. Fast, minimal bandwidth.

API Families Explained

API Protocol When to Use Developer Analogy

OData OASIS OData v4 Legacy metadata search SQL-like query syntax over HTTP

STAC: Finding Your Data

Before processing anything, you need to know what's available. STAC returns JSON describing collections and items:

STAC Query Example 
// Search for cloud-free Sentinel-2 images over a lake
const response = await fetch('https://stac.dataspace.copernicus.eu/v1/search', {
  method: 'POST',
  headers: { 'Content-Type': 'application/json' },
  body: JSON.stringify({
    collections: ['SENTINEL2_L2A'],
    bbox: [11.2, 44.1, 11.5, 44.3],  // west, south, east, north
    datetime: '2026-04-01/2026-04-08',
    query: {
      'eo:cloud_cover': { 'lt': 20 }  // Less than 20% clouds
    },
    limit: 10
  })
});

const data = await response.json();
// Returns: { features: [{ id: 'S2A_...', geometry: {...}, properties: {...}, assets: {...} }] }

💡 Why Filter at Catalog Level?

Cloud filtering in STAC saves processing credits. If you request a processing job for a cloudy day, you'll pay for computation but get useless results. Filter first, then process only useful scenes.

Sentinel Hub APIs

Sentinel Hub is the workhorse for real-time satellite data processing. Its core abstraction is the evalscript.

Evalscripts: Server-Side Pixel Programs

An evalscript is a JavaScript function that defines how each pixel should be computed. Think of it as a serverless function that runs on every pixel in your area of interest:

Evalscript Structure 
//VERSION=3

function setup() {
  // Declare which data sources and bands you need
  return {
    input: [{
      bands: ["B03", "B08"],     // Green and NIR bands
      units: "REFLECTANCE"       // We want 0-1 reflectance, not raw DN
    }],
    output: {
      bands: 1,                   // Output a single-band image
      sampleType: "FLOAT32"       // Floating point for index values
    }
  };
}

function evaluatePixel(sample) {
  // This runs for EVERY PIXEL in your AOI
  // sample.B03 = green reflectance, sample.B08 = NIR reflectance
  
  let ndwi = (sample.B03 - sample.B08) / (sample.B03 + sample.B08);
  
  // Return NDWI value. Values > ~0.42 indicate water.
  return [ndwi];
}

Processing API

Generates images or numeric rasters on the fly. Send evalscript + geometry + time range, get back PNG/TIFF/raw data:

Processing API Request 
const request = {
  input: {
    bounds: {
      bbox: [11.2, 44.1, 11.5, 44.3],
      properties: { crs: "http://www.opengis.net/def/crs/EPSG/0/4326" }
    },
    data: [{
      dataFilter: {
        timeRange: { from: "2026-04-01", to: "2026-04-08" },
        maxCloudCoverage: 20
      },
      type: "sentinel-2-l2a"
    }]
  },
  output: {
    width: 512,
    height: 512,
    responses: [{ identifier: "default", format: { type: "image/tiff" } }]
  },
  evalscript: `...your evalscript here...`
};

const response = await axios.post(
  'https://sh.dataspace.copernicus.eu/process/v1',
  request,
  { headers: { 'Authorization': `Bearer ${token}` }, responseType: 'arraybuffer' }
);
// response.data is a GeoTIFF binary

📊 sampleType Explained

FLOAT32: For precise index values (-1 to +1). Use for NDWI, Chlorophyll.
UINT16: For integer values (0-65535). Use when mapping to discrete classes or for compact storage.

Data Fusion: Combining Multiple Sources

A single evalscript can ingest multiple collections (e.g., Sentinel-1 + Sentinel-2):

Multi-Source Evalscript 
//VERSION=3
function setup() {
  return {
    input: [
      { datasource: "S2L2A", bands: ["B02", "B03", "B04", "SCL"] },
      { datasource: "S1GRD", bands: ["VV", "VH"] }
    ],
    output: { bands: 3, sampleType: "AUTO" }
  };
}

function evaluatePixel(samples) {
  let s2 = samples.S2L2A[0];  // Optical data
  let s1 = samples.S1GRD[0];  // Radar data
  
  // Flood detection: SAR backscatter < threshold AND not cloud in optical
  let isFlood = (s1.VV < 0.0316) && (s2.SCL !== 8 && s2.SCL !== 9);
  
  if (isFlood) {
    return [0.1, 0.4, 0.9];  // Blue for flooded areas
  } else {
    return [s2.B04 * 2.5, s2.B03 * 2.5, s2.B02 * 2.5];  // True color
  }
}

openEO Datacube Analytics

openEO provides a higher-level abstraction than Sentinel Hub - instead of pixel-level evalscripts, you work with datacubes and process graphs.

What's a Datacube?

A datacube is a multi-dimensional array indexed by:

  • X, Y (spatial dimensions)
  • T (temporal dimension - multiple dates)
  • Bands (spectral dimension - B02, B03, B08, etc.)

This abstraction hides storage details. You write operations like "reduce temporal dimension by mean" and the backend optimizes execution.

Python Workflow

openEO Python Example 
import openeo

# 1. Connect and authenticate
connection = openeo.connect("https://openeo.dataspace.copernicus.eu")
connection.authenticate_oidc()

# 2. Load datacube (lazy - no data fetched yet)
cube = connection.load_collection(
    "SENTINEL2_L2A",
    spatial_extent={"west": 11.2, "south": 44.1, "east": 11.5, "north": 44.3},
    temporal_extent=["2026-04-01", "2026-04-08"],
    bands=["B03", "B04", "B08", "SCL"]
)

# 3. Cloud masking using Scene Classification Layer (SCL)
# mask_scl_dilation is a CDSE-specific optimization
cube = cube.process("mask_scl_dilation", data=cube, scl_band_name="SCL")

# 4. Reduce time dimension → single cloud-free composite
composite = cube.reduce_dimension(dimension="t", reducer="mean")

# 5. Execute (NOW data is processed and downloaded)
composite.download("water_composite.tif")  # For small areas
# OR
job = composite.execute_batch()             # For large areas (async)

User-Defined Functions (UDFs)

When standard processes aren't enough, inject custom Python code via UDFs:

UDF Example 
# UDF that runs on the backend
def apply_udf(cube: XarrayDataCube) -> XarrayDataCube:
    import numpy as np
    import xarray as xr
    
    array = cube.get_array()
    
    # Custom water quality calculation
    b03 = array.sel(bands="B03")
    b08 = array.sel(bands="B08")
    
    ndwi = (b03 - b08) / (b03 + b08)
    
    # Classify: 0 = land, 1 = clear water, 2 = turbid water
    classified = xr.where(ndwi < 0.3, 0, xr.where(ndwi < 0.6, 2, 1))
    
    return XarrayDataCube(classified)

# In your workflow:
cube = cube.apply(process=openeo.UDF(apply_udf))

Spectral Analysis Explained

Understanding why we use specific band combinations requires knowing how different materials reflect light.

The Physics of Remote Sensing

🌊 Why Water Looks Dark in NIR

Water strongly absorbs near-infrared radiation. When the satellite measures NIR (B08), water appears very dark while vegetation and soil appear bright. This contrast is the foundation of water detection indices.

Reflectance Signatures

Every material has a unique "spectral signature" - how it reflects different wavelengths:

Material Blue (B02) Green (B03) Red (B04) NIR (B08)
Clear Water Low Low Very Low ~0 (absorbed)
Turbid Water Medium Medium Medium Low
Algal Bloom Low High (green pigment) Low Medium (cell scatter)
Vegetation Low Medium Low Very High
Bare Soil Medium Medium Medium-High High

Why Band Ratios Work

Instead of using absolute reflectance values (which vary with sun angle, atmosphere, etc.), we use ratios between bands. This normalizes out many environmental factors:

Index Formula Pattern 
// Normalized Difference indices follow this pattern:
index = (band_that_reflects - band_that_absorbs) / (band_that_reflects + band_that_absorbs)

// Result ranges from -1 to +1
// High positive = target material (e.g., water for NDWI)
// High negative = opposite material (e.g., vegetation for NDWI)
// Near zero = mixed or ambiguous

Red-Edge: The Algae Detection Sweet Spot

Bands B05-B07 (705-783nm) capture the "red-edge" - a steep rise in vegetation/algae reflectance:

🔬 Phycocyanin Detection

Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) contain a pigment called phycocyanin that creates a reflectance "bump" between 705-740nm. This signature distinguishes toxic cyanobacteria from harmless green algae. The Maximum Peak Height (MPH) index exploits this.

Water Quality Indices

These indices convert raw reflectance values into physically meaningful measurements.

NDWI: Water Detection

Normalized Difference Water Index

NDWI = (B03 - B08) / (B03 + B08)

Green (560nm) - NIR (842nm) / Green + NIR

NDWI Value Interpretation Action
> 0.42 Definitely water Apply biochemical indices
0.2 - 0.42 Mixed pixel or wet soil Flag as uncertain
< 0.2 Land, vegetation, built-up Exclude from water analysis

⚠️ Threshold Tuning

The 0.42 threshold works for most clear water. Highly turbid or shallow water may need lower thresholds (~0.3). Validate against known water bodies in your AOI.

Biochemical Indicators

The Se2WaQ (Sentinel-2 Water Quality) evalscript computes six indicators from empirical models. These are NOT direct measurements - they're statistical correlations validated against field samples.

Indicator Reference

Indicator What It Measures Formula Unit
Chlorophyll-a Algae concentration (primary productivity) 4.26 × (B03/B01)^3.94 mg/m³
Cyanobacteria Blue-green algae cell density (toxic risk) 115530.31 × (B03×B04/B02)^2.38 10³ cells/ml
Turbidity Water cloudiness (suspended particles) 8.93 × (B03/B01) - 6.39 NTU
CDOM Colored Dissolved Organic Matter (brownish tint) 537 × exp(-2.93 × B03/B04) mg/l
DOC Dissolved Organic Carbon 432 × exp(-2.24 × B03/B04) mg/l
Water Color Apparent color (Pt-Co scale) 25366 × exp(-4.53 × B03/B04) mg Pt/l

Evalscript Implementation

Se2WaQ Evalscript (Simplified) 
//VERSION=3
function setup() {
  return {
    input: [{ bands: ["B01", "B02", "B03", "B04", "B08"] }],
    output: [
      { id: "ndwi", bands: 1, sampleType: "FLOAT32" },
      { id: "chlorophyll", bands: 1, sampleType: "FLOAT32" },
      { id: "turbidity", bands: 1, sampleType: "FLOAT32" },
      { id: "cyanobacteria", bands: 1, sampleType: "FLOAT32" }
    ]
  };
}

function evaluatePixel(sample) {
  // Step 1: Water detection
  let ndwi = (sample.B03 - sample.B08) / (sample.B03 + sample.B08);
  
  // Only compute biochemical indices for water pixels
  if (ndwi < 0.42) {
    return { ndwi: [ndwi], chlorophyll: [NaN], turbidity: [NaN], cyanobacteria: [NaN] };
  }
  
  // Step 2: Biochemical indicators (only for water)
  let chl_a = 4.26 * Math.pow(sample.B03 / sample.B01, 3.94);
  let turb = 8.93 * (sample.B03 / sample.B01) - 6.39;
  let cya = 115530.31 * Math.pow((sample.B03 * sample.B04) / sample.B02, 2.38);
  
  return {
    ndwi: [ndwi],
    chlorophyll: [chl_a],
    turbidity: [turb],
    cyanobacteria: [cya]
  };
}

Trophic State Index (TSI)

Sentinel-3 OLCI provides the Trophic State Index for large lakes (>50 hectares), classifying ecological health:

0-40 Oligotrophic Clear water, low nutrients, low algae. Healthy drinking water source.
40-60 Mesotrophic Moderate clarity, some algae. Normal for many lakes.
60-80 Eutrophic High nutrients, frequent algal blooms. Swimming advisories possible.
80-100 Hypereutrophic Severe nutrient overload, toxic blooms likely. Public health risk.

CEMS & GloFAS Flood Monitoring

The Copernicus Emergency Management Service (CEMS) provides flood forecasting and monitoring. AquaSync uses these to distinguish pollution from natural hydrological events.

Service Architecture

Service Type Resolution Use Case
GloFAS Forecasting (up to 30 days) 0.05° (~5 km) Probabilistic flood prediction, return period thresholds
GFM Monitoring (near-real-time) 20 m Current flood extent from Sentinel-1 SAR

GloFAS: Flood Forecasting

GloFAS couples ECMWF weather forecasts with the LISFLOOD hydrological model:

🌊 Return Period

A "10-year return period flood" means the discharge level that statistically occurs once every 10 years. GloFAS provides thresholds for 2, 5, 10, 20-year events.

When river discharge exceeds the 2-year threshold, AquaSync adjusts the Pollution Severity Index because high flows dilute pollutants.

GFM: SAR-Based Flood Monitoring

Global Flood Monitoring uses Sentinel-1 radar to detect water extent regardless of clouds:

🎯 Why This Matters for AquaSync

If high turbidity coincides with a GFM-detected flood, AquaSync classifies it as "Hydrological Runoff" instead of "Point-Source Pollution". This dramatically reduces false alarms for regulators.

SAR Analysis for Water Detection

Synthetic Aperture Radar works differently from optical sensors and requires understanding of backscatter physics.

How SAR "Sees" Water

Smooth water = specular reflection = dark pixels
The radar pulse bounces away from the sensor like a mirror. Very little signal returns.

Rough surfaces = diffuse reflection = bright pixels
The radar pulse scatters in all directions, including back to the sensor.

The Urban Double-Bounce Problem

In cities, flooded areas can appear BRIGHT (not dark) due to "double-bounce" reflection:

  1. Radar hits water surface
  2. Bounces to vertical building wall
  3. Returns straight back to sensor
  4. Result: Very strong signal despite water presence

This is why urban flood mapping requires data fusion with optical imagery or interferometric coherence.

HASARD Algorithm

The GFM system uses HASARD (Hierarchical Split-based Approach for Automatic Rapid Detection):

HASARD Processing Flow 
1. Hierarchical Splitting
   └── Recursively divide SAR image until homogeneous backscatter (σ⁰_VV) in each tile

2. Statistical Modeling
   └── Compare current acquisition vs same-orbit reference baseline
   └── Identify pixels with significant backscatter decrease = new water

3. Region Growing
   └── Use detected water pixels as seeds
   └── Grow regions to capture complex plume shapes across tile boundaries

4. Comparison with Reference Water Mask
   └── Distinguish permanent water bodies from temporary flooding

Galileo High Accuracy Service (HAS)

HAS provides decimeter-level positioning without ground infrastructure - critical for remote areas.

How HAS Works

📡 Precise Point Positioning (PPP)

Standard GPS computes position from signal travel time, limited by satellite orbit/clock errors and atmospheric delays.

PPP uses correction data (precise orbits, clocks, biases) to remove these errors. Traditional PPP services send corrections via internet. HAS broadcasts corrections directly from Galileo satellites on the E6-B signal.

Performance Specs

Metric Service Level 1 (Global) Service Level 2 (Europe)
Horizontal Accuracy (95%) < 20 cm < 20 cm
Vertical Accuracy (95%) < 40 cm < 40 cm
Convergence Time < 300 seconds < 100 seconds (with ionospheric corrections)

💡 Why This Matters

In disaster zones, cellular networks are often down. RTK systems fail. But HAS keeps working because corrections come directly from space - no ground infrastructure needed.

OSNMA: Anti-Spoofing Authentication

Open Service Navigation Message Authentication adds cryptographic proof that GNSS signals are genuine.

The Spoofing Threat

⚠️ Why Authentication Matters

A sophisticated attacker can broadcast fake GNSS signals that overpower real ones. Your receiver reports a false location. For AquaSync, this could let someone report pollution at wrong coordinates to misdirect enforcement or hide illegal discharges.

TESLA Protocol Explained

OSNMA uses delayed key disclosure - the key to verify a message is revealed AFTER the message is already received:

1

MAC Sent

Satellite sends navigation data + MAC (Message Authentication Code). You can't verify it yet.

→
2

Key Disclosed (30s later)

Satellite reveals the key used to compute that MAC. Now you can verify.

→
3

Chain Verification

Hash the key recursively until it matches the trusted Root Key you got from ESA.

The genius: An attacker can't forge the MAC without the key, but by the time the key is revealed, the spoofed message is already obsolete.

Receiver Requirements

  • OSNMA-capable hardware (u-blox F9, Septentrio mosaic-X5, etc.)
  • Root public key from European GNSS Service Centre (GSC)
  • Synchronization to Galileo System Time within 30-300 seconds

Proportional Grid Calculation

When you need to aggregate pollution values to a regular grid:

Area-Weighted Grid Cell Value

Vgrid = Σ(intersection_area × polygon_value) / grid_cell_area

PostGIS Grid Aggregation 
-- Calculate area-weighted pollution index per grid cell
SELECT 
  grid.id,
  grid.geom,
  SUM(ST_Area(ST_Intersection(grid.geom, poly.geom)) * poly.pollution_value) 
    / ST_Area(grid.geom) AS weighted_value
FROM target_grid grid
JOIN pollution_polygons poly 
  ON ST_Intersects(grid.geom, poly.geom)
GROUP BY grid.id, grid.geom;

const axios = require('axios');

// Token cache
let accessToken = null;
let tokenExpiry = 0;

async function getToken() {
  if (accessToken && Date.now() < tokenExpiry - 60000) {
    return accessToken;  // Return cached token if not expiring soon
  }
  
  const response = await axios.post(
    'https://identity.dataspace.copernicus.eu/auth/realms/CDSE/protocol/openid-connect/token',
    new URLSearchParams({
      grant_type: 'client_credentials',
      client_id: process.env.CDSE_CLIENT_ID,
      client_secret: process.env.CDSE_SECRET
    }),
    { headers: { 'Content-Type': 'application/x-www-form-urlencoded' } }
  );
  
  accessToken = response.data.access_token;
  tokenExpiry = Date.now() + (response.data.expires_in * 1000);
  return accessToken;
}

// AXIOS instance with auto-refresh
const cdseClient = axios.create({
  baseURL: 'https://sh.dataspace.copernicus.eu'
});

cdseClient.interceptors.request.use(async (config) => {
  const token = await getToken();
  config.headers.Authorization = `Bearer ${token}`;
  return config;
});

cdseClient.interceptors.response.use(
  (response) => response,
  async (error) => {
    if (error.response?.status === 401 && !error.config._retry) {
      error.config._retry = true;
      accessToken = null;  // Force refresh
      const token = await getToken();
      error.config.headers.Authorization = `Bearer ${token}`;
      return cdseClient(error.config);
    }
    return Promise.reject(error);
  }
);

Physical Reasoning

Gemini goes beyond object detection to infer material properties and context:

🧠 Attribute Inference

"The liquid is observed to be highly opaque with an iridescent surface. The physicality of the discharge is consistent with oil-contaminated effluent rather than natural biogenic oils."

The model reasons about viscosity, color consistency, and surface behavior based on its world knowledge.

Temporal Consistency Check

The AI cross-references photo content with metadata:

  1. Analyze shadow angles and lighting in the photo
  2. Infer approximate time of day
  3. Compare against reported GNSS timestamp
  4. Calculate expected solar azimuth for that location/date
  5. Flag mismatches (e.g., evening shadows at 10 AM timestamp)

Fraud Detection

A common fraud technique is re-photographing a screen to submit historical images as "live" evidence.

Moiré Pattern Detection

🔬 What is Moiré?

When a camera photographs a screen, the camera's sensor grid interferes with the display's pixel grid, creating wavy interference patterns called moiré. These are invisible to the naked eye but detectable by algorithms.

Detection Metrics

Metric Calculation Fraud Signature
Sharpness Std deviation of Laplacian-filtered grayscale Anomalously high values in areas that should be smooth (sky, water)
Colorfulness Avg std deviation of LAB A/B channels Characteristic chromatic aberration from screen subpixels
Frequency Analysis FFT spectrum analysis Periodic patterns at screen refresh frequencies
Sharpness Metric (Python) 
import cv2
import numpy as np

def calculate_sharpness(image_path):
    """High sharpness in smooth areas = potential screen capture"""
    img = cv2.imread(image_path, cv2.IMREAD_GRAYSCALE)
    laplacian = cv2.Laplacian(img, cv2.CV_64F)
    return laplacian.std()

def calculate_colorfulness(image_path):
    """Screen captures have characteristic LAB color distortion"""
    img = cv2.imread(image_path)
    lab = cv2.cvtColor(img, cv2.COLOR_BGR2LAB)
    a_std = np.std(lab[:,:,1])
    b_std = np.std(lab[:,:,2])
    return (a_std + b_std) / 2

SHARPNESS_THRESHOLD = 150  # Typical natural photo: 50-100
COLORFULNESS_THRESHOLD = 40  # Typical natural photo: 20-35

Glossary

Quick reference for terms used throughout this documentation.

AOI (Area of Interest)

The geographic region you're analyzing. Usually specified as a bounding box (west, south, east, north) or GeoJSON polygon.

Backscatter (σ⁰)

The amount of radar signal that returns to the sensor. Water is low, rough surfaces are high. Measured in decibels (dB).

BOA (Bottom-of-Atmosphere)

Reflectance at Earth's surface after atmospheric correction. Use Level-2A products for BOA.

CDOM

Colored Dissolved Organic Matter. Dissolved substances that give water a brownish/yellowish tint. Indicator of organic pollution.

Evalscript

JavaScript code that runs server-side on Sentinel Hub, defining how to process each pixel. Similar to a serverless function.

GRD (Ground Range Detected)

Sentinel-1 SAR product format. Pixels represent radar backscatter projected onto the ground surface.

L2A (Level-2A)

Sentinel-2 product with atmospheric correction applied. Contains surface reflectance instead of top-of-atmosphere radiance.

NDWI

Normalized Difference Water Index. (Green - NIR) / (Green + NIR). Values >0.42 typically indicate water.

NIR (Near-Infrared)

Wavelengths around 700-1100nm. Invisible to humans, strongly absorbed by water, strongly reflected by vegetation.

OSNMA

Open Service Navigation Message Authentication. Galileo's cryptographic protection against GPS/GNSS spoofing.

PPP (Precise Point Positioning)

GNSS technique using satellite-broadcast corrections to achieve cm-level accuracy without base stations.

Red-Edge

Wavelengths 700-750nm where vegetation/algae reflectance rises steeply. Critical for detecting algal blooms.

SAR (Synthetic Aperture Radar)

Radar imaging that works through clouds and at night. Measures surface roughness and moisture.

SCL (Scene Classification Layer)

Sentinel-2 L2A band classifying each pixel as cloud, shadow, vegetation, water, etc. Used for masking.

STAC

SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog. Standard JSON format for describing geospatial data collections and items.

TOA (Top-of-Atmosphere)

Radiance measured at satellite altitude, before atmospheric correction. Use Level-1C products.

TSI (Trophic State Index)

0-100 scale measuring lake biological productivity. Higher = more algae = more pollution risk.

Turbidity (NTU)

Nephelometric Turbidity Units. Measures water cloudiness from suspended particles. Higher = more particles.
